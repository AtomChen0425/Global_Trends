Long Video Question Answering (LVQA) presents a significant challenge due to the sheer volume of data in hours-long untrimmed videos. Current methods struggle with either excessive computational demands from dense processing with large Vision-Language Models (VLMs) or miss crucial, temporally localized, and motion-centric evidence by relying solely on sparse caption-based reasoning. This necessitates a more efficient and effective approach to identify relevant evidence for answering queries within these extensive video datasets.

**Technical Implementation**

TimeProVe addresses these limitations through a cost-efficient hybrid framework. Its core innovation is the Action-based Candidate Evidence (ACE) module. ACE leverages lightweight LLM reasoning to transform temporally localized actions into query-conditioned candidate answers and their supporting evidence windows. This module acts as a crucial pre-filtering step, generating hypotheses without the high cost of full VLM processing. Subsequently, a powerful, but expensive, VLM is invoked only for targeted verification of these generated hypotheses. This staged approach significantly reduces computational overhead.

**Application Scenarios**

The framework is particularly well-suited for scenarios requiring temporally grounded reasoning in real-world activities. The introduction of OpenTSUBench (OTB), an open-ended benchmark for Activities of Daily Living (ADL), highlights its practical applicability. Experiments demonstrate TimeProVe's effectiveness on OTB, outperforming existing baselines by a notable margin while drastically cutting down VLM calls and overall inference costs. Furthermore, its ability to achieve competitive results on datasets like Charades-STA, even without explicit temporal grounding training, underscores its robust generalization capabilities and potential for state-of-the-art performance when integrated with grounding VLMs.

**Summary**

TimeProVe offers a practical and computationally efficient solution for Long Video Question Answering by intelligently combining lightweight action-based reasoning with targeted VLM verification. Its ACE module effectively identifies candidate evidence, significantly reducing the reliance on expensive dense VLM processing. The framework's demonstrated performance improvements and cost reductions, particularly in real-world ADL scenarios evaluated on OTB, position it as a promising advancement in the field of temporally grounded video understanding.

Egocentric video understanding is inherently limited by the narrow perspective of wearable cameras: a single viewpoint, a single modality, a single model cannot capture the full richness of human action. We argue that a truly expressive egocentric representation must subsume complementary knowledge across viewpoints, modalities, and foundation model representations, yet remain deployable from egocentric video alone. To this end, we introduce a hierarchical multi-teacher distillation framework that produces UNIEGO, a unified egocentric encoder trained with nine teachers spanning ego-exo viewpoints, RGB, depth, and skeleton modalities, and four foundation models. Rather than distilling directly from heterogeneous teachers whose incompatible architectures and feature geometries induce conflicting gradients, our framework interposes a layer of representation-specific Proxy models that translate diverse teacher knowledge into a homogeneous egocentric space. SPD is further stabilized by initializing UNIEGO as a learned convex combination of proxy parameters, placing the unified model in a well-conditioned region of the loss landscape before distillation begins. UNIEGO achieves state-of-the-art performance across three egocentric video understanding tasks - action recognition, video retrieval, and action segmentation on three challenging ego-exo benchmarks, outperforming naive multi-teacher distillation baselines and demonstrating that structured, proxy-mediated knowledge transfer yields richer and more discriminative egocentric representations.

**Background**

This work introduces "Lie-Algebra Attention," a novel attention mechanism where tokens are directly represented as elements of a matrix Lie group. Unlike traditional methods that rely on learned kernels or feature-rich tokens, Lie-Algebra Attention uses bare group transformations. The core innovation lies in its scoring mechanism, which leverages the intrinsic geometric properties of these group elements. Specifically, the pairwise attention score is derived from the closed-form algebra norm of the relative pose between tokens, eliminating the need for complex representation theory or learned components for this calculation.

**Technical Implementation**

The attention score, $s_{ij}$, is defined as the negative squared algebra norm of the logarithm of the relative pose: $s_{ij} = -\|\log(g_i^{-1} g_j)\|_λ^2/τ$. This approach inherently captures the geometric relationship between tokens. The use of the logarithm maps relative poses into the Lie algebra, allowing for a canonical and intrinsic measure of proximity. This construction is designed to be naturally equivariant under diagonal group actions and automatically satisfies the cocycle condition, simplifying the theoretical underpinnings. Crucially, this method is applicable to a broad range of matrix Lie groups, including non-compact and non-abelian groups like affine transformations (Aff(2)), which are typically challenging for vector-token based attention mechanisms.

**Application Scenarios**

The practical efficacy of Lie-Algebra Attention is demonstrated through sequence completion experiments on SE(2), SO(3), and Aff(2). The results indicate that the closed-form, geometrically derived score performs competitively with, and in some cases outperforms, learned MLP kernels operating on the same invariant features. A significant advantage highlighted is the drastic reduction in model parameters, with Lie-Algebra Attention requiring 50 to 80 times fewer score parameters. Furthermore, it exhibits superior invariance properties compared to vector-token baselines, which suffer significant invariance breakdown. This suggests strong potential for applications requiring robust geometric reasoning and efficient parameterization, particularly in domains involving rigid body transformations, pose estimation, and motion modeling.

**Summary**

Lie-Algebra Attention presents a paradigm shift in attention mechanisms by grounding tokens in matrix Lie group elements and employing a geometrically derived, closed-form scoring function. The experimental validation on sequence completion tasks underscores its practical viability and superior performance in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency.
