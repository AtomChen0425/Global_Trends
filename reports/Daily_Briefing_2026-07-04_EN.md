This article introduces Multi-Object Generative Perception (MultiGP), a novel generative inverse rendering technique designed to disentangle and sample the radiometric properties of objects from a single image. The core challenge addressed is the inherent ambiguity in separating reflectance, texture, and illumination when only a single view is available. MultiGP's foundational insight is to exploit the common illumination shared by all objects within a scene, even if their individual textures and reflectances vary.

**Technical Implementation**

MultiGP achieves this radiometric disentanglement through a four-pronged technical approach. It employs a cascaded end-to-end architecture that integrates both image-space and angular-space disentanglement strategies. To ensure a consistent illumination estimate across multiple objects, a "Coordinated Scheduling" mechanism is utilized to guide diffusion model convergence. "Axial Attention" is incorporated to enable effective interaction and information sharing between objects with differing reflectance properties. Finally, a "Texture Extraction ControlNet" is implemented to preserve fine-grained texture details while ensuring their independence from the estimated lighting conditions.

**Application Scenarios**

The practical implications of MultiGP are significant for applications requiring detailed scene understanding and manipulation. By accurately recovering individual object textures and reflectances alongside the common scene illumination, MultiGP can facilitate advanced image editing, virtual object insertion with realistic lighting, and photorealistic rendering. Its ability to disentangle these radiometric components from a single image opens doors for more efficient and robust 3D content creation and scene reconstruction pipelines.

**Summary**

In essence, MultiGP presents a sophisticated generative inverse rendering framework that tackles the complex problem of radiometric disentanglement. By leveraging the shared illumination characteristic of scenes and employing a suite of innovative architectural components and attention mechanisms, it successfully recovers individual object appearance properties and common lighting from single images. This advancement holds considerable promise for enhancing realism and control in various computer vision and graphics applications.
