Ink3D is proposed as a solution to bridge this gap, aiming to leverage the power of large-scale video generative models for enhanced 3D texture synthesis.

This model generates dense, multi-view "orbit-scan" videos of the object. **Summary**

Ink3D presents a compelling framework for generating complex 3D textures by synergistically combining 3D geometry reconstruction with large-scale video generative models. Its key technical contributions include the use of OrbitPainter for multi-view video synthesis and TextureOptimizer for robust neural texture baking. This approach effectively addresses the data scarcity issue in 3D texture generation, promising richer and more accurate results for demanding visual applications.

Most recent advances in 3D generative modeling rely on diffusion or flow-matching formulations. We instead explore a fully autoregressive alternative and introduce GaussianGPT, a transformer-based model that directly generates 3D Gaussians via next-token prediction, thus facilitating full 3D scene generation. We first compress Gaussian primitives into a discrete latent grid using a sparse 3D convolutional autoencoder with vector quantization. The resulting tokens are serialized and modeled using a causal transformer with 3D rotary positional embedding, enabling sequential generation of spatial structure and appearance. Unlike diffusion-based methods that refine scenes holistically, our formulation constructs scenes step-by-step, naturally supporting completion, outpainting, controllable sampling via temperature, and flexible generation horizons. This formulation leverages the compositional inductive biases and scalability of autoregressive modeling while operating on explicit representations compatible with modern neural rendering pipelines, positioning autoregressive transformers as a complementary paradigm for controllable and context-aware 3D generation.

**Background**

This work introduces Linkify, a novel framework for context-aware part retrieval in mechanical assemblies. Traditional generative AI approaches for CAD often focus on individual components or complete, static assemblies. Linkify addresses a critical gap by leveraging the underutilized geometric information present at the interfaces between parts, which is fundamental to assembly function. The research recomputes and enhances interface geometry from the Fusion 360 Gallery Assembly dataset, ensuring accurate contact information.

**Technical Implementation**

Linkify constructs assembly graphs where nodes represent part geometry and edges capture interface geometry. This interface data is encoded as point-cloud representations using a pretrained point-cloud encoder. A Graph Attention Network (GATv2) is then trained on this graph structure for a masked part prediction task. This task simulates a realistic part-retrieval scenario by requiring the model to predict a missing component based on its surrounding context within the assembly. Key to Linkify's success are accurate contact computation and dynamic attention mechanisms that focus on the critical interface regions.

**Application Scenarios**

The primary application demonstrated is context-aware part retrieval, enabling systems to suggest appropriate components based on their intended placement and interaction within an assembly. This has significant implications for design assistance, automated assembly planning, and potentially for generative design systems that can propose novel assembly configurations. The framework's ability to understand functional relationships through interface geometry opens doors for more intelligent and context-aware CAD tools.

**Summary**

Linkify presents a significant advancement in understanding and leveraging interface geometry for mechanical assembly analysis. By representing assemblies as graphs with rich edge attributes encoding contact information, and employing advanced graph neural networks, the framework achieves superior performance in part retrieval tasks compared to non-graph-based methods. The public release of the corrected interface dataset and training pipeline provides a valuable resource for future research in interface-aware assembly modeling, validation, and generative design.

We present World from Motion, a method for generating freely renderable dynamic 3D Gaussian representations from monocular videos. Our approach conditions a video model on dense, pixel-aligned renderings that encode appearance, geometry, and 3D scene motion along both input and target camera trajectories to correct rendering artifacts and fill in missing regions from an initial reconstruction.
